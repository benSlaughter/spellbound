# Game Design Research — What Makes Kids Come Back

Research notes for SpellBound. What the science says, what the industry does, what we should do.

---

## The Two Sides of Engagement

There's a clear line between games that are **engaging** and games that are **exploitative**. The same psychological principles power both — the difference is intent and design choices.

### What Roblox/Fortnite do (and we won't)

The Sydney University study (March 2025) found that Roblox's monetisation causes real harm to children:
- **Virtual currency obfuscation** — kids can't grasp real-world costs ("I can't even begin to grasp that" — 13-year-old on Robux pricing)
- **Loot boxes / random rewards** — still present despite Australia's 2024 ban for under-15s
- **FOMO mechanics** — limited-time events, daily login streaks that punish absence
- **Social pressure** — rare items as status symbols, peer comparison
- **Compulsion loops** — endless progression that makes stopping feel like losing

These are **dark patterns**. They work by exploiting developing brains. We don't do any of this.

### What we CAN learn from them

The underlying psychology is valid — it's the application that matters. Here are the frameworks.

## Framework 1: Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

The most researched motivation framework in game design. Three core human needs:

### Autonomy — "I choose this"
- Let kids pick which game to play, which words to practise, which area to explore
- Never force a specific path or sequence
- Meaningful choices within games (not just cosmetic)
- **SpellBound today:** ✅ Free choice of games from hub pages
- **Could improve:** Let kids choose game themes, pick their garden layout, set their own goals

### Competence — "I'm getting better"
- Progressive difficulty that grows with the child
- Clear, immediate feedback on every action
- Visible mastery — not scores, but evidence of growth
- Safe failure — wrong answers are learning, not punishment
- **SpellBound today:** ✅ Encouraging feedback, no penalties, celebrations
- **Could improve:** Adaptive difficulty based on accuracy, visible skill progression per word/table

### Relatedness — "I belong"
- Connection to characters, world, or other people
- Feeling part of something bigger
- Investment in a persistent world that responds to you
- **SpellBound today:** ⚠️ Weakest area — garden grows but feels passive
- **Could improve:** Garden creatures that react to you, character with personality, family mode where siblings see each other's gardens

## Framework 2: Flow Theory (Csikszentmihalyi)

The "flow channel" — the sweet spot between boredom and frustration where engagement peaks.

```
Anxiety/Frustration
     ↑
     |     ╱ FLOW
     |   ╱   CHANNEL
     | ╱
     ╱
Boredom → Skill Level →
```

### How to maintain flow:
1. **Match challenge to skill** — too easy = bored, too hard = frustrated
2. **Clear goals** — always know what to do next
3. **Immediate feedback** — instant response to every action
4. **Sense of control** — player feels in charge, not dragged along

### Applied to SpellBound:
- **Today:** Games are fixed difficulty. A kid who knows "cat" and a kid who knows "photosynthesis" get the same experience.
- **Opportunity:** Adaptive difficulty — track accuracy per word/fact, surface harder items more, easier items less. This is spaced repetition (#12) combined with flow theory.

## Framework 3: The Hook Model (Nir Eyal)

A 4-step cycle for building habits. Can be used ethically or exploitatively.

### The Cycle:
1. **Trigger** → What prompts the child to open the app?
2. **Action** → What's the simplest thing they do first?
3. **Variable Reward** → What surprises them?
4. **Investment** → What do they put in that makes them want to come back?

### Ethical application for SpellBound:

| Step | Dark Pattern (avoid) | Ethical Version (do this) |
|------|---------------------|--------------------------|
| **Trigger** | Push notifications, FOMO streaks | Parent says "time for spellings" + app feels welcoming |
| **Action** | Forced daily login quests | One-tap into a game, zero friction |
| **Variable Reward** | Loot boxes, random prizes | New garden creatures appearing, surprise encouragement messages, unexpected badge unlocks |
| **Investment** | Sunk cost (money spent) | Garden that grows, words mastered, personal bests |

## What the Successful EdTech Games Do

### Prodigy Math (millions of monthly users)
- RPG wrapper — you're a wizard battling monsters with maths
- Curriculum-aligned questions adjust to skill level
- Adventure narrative gives context to every problem
- Daily quests provide gentle structure (but no punishment for missing)
- Parent dashboard shows real learning data

### Duolingo (most successful learning app ever)
- 5-minute sessions — absurdly low friction
- Streaks that celebrate consistency (but added "streak freeze" to reduce punishment anxiety)
- Hearts system creates gentle stakes (limited attempts, but replenishable)
- Leaderboards for competitive kids, optional for others
- Bite-sized progression — every session ends with visible progress

### DragonBox (algebra disguised as puzzles)
- Abstract concepts made tangible through visual manipulation
- Zero maths notation until the child already understands the concept
- Progressive revelation — the "game" slowly transforms into "real maths"
- No failure state — just different paths to the solution

## The 7 Patterns That Actually Work (Ethically)

Based on all the research, here's what makes a kids' learning game genuinely replayable:

### 1. Variable Novelty (not Variable Rewards)
Don't randomise prizes — randomise the *experience*. Different words each session, different garden events, surprise creatures, seasonal changes. The child doesn't know exactly what they'll see, but they're never manipulated.

### 2. Visible Growth Over Time
The garden is our strongest asset. A child who can SEE their progress — flowers that weren't there yesterday, a tree that's taller, a bee that appeared — has a reason to come back that isn't a streak counter or a points score. Make the garden MORE responsive to play.

### 3. Completion Drive (not Collection Pressure)
Kids naturally want to "finish things." Let them complete a spelling list across multiple game types. Let them master a times table. Show a gentle progress wheel that fills up — not a leaderboard that ranks them.

### 4. Effortless Entry
One tap from home to playing. No loading screens, no menus, no setup. The moment of "I want to play" to "I'm playing" must be < 3 seconds. This is Duolingo's single greatest insight.

### 5. Safe Failure + Gentle Scaffolding
Wrong answers should be *interesting*, not punishing. Show a wobble, give a hint, let them try again. After 3 wrong attempts, help them — don't fail them. The child should never feel stupid. This is the single most important design principle for a 10-year-old.

### 6. Surprise and Delight
Unexpected positive moments. A butterfly lands on a flower you grew. An achievement unlocks that you didn't know existed. A new game appears in the hub. The encouragement messages rotate so you don't see the same one twice. These small delights create emotional memories.

### 7. Gentle Rhythm (not Streaks)
Instead of punishing breaks with lost streaks, *celebrate returns*. "Welcome back! Your garden missed you — look, a new butterfly arrived while you were away!" The app should feel like a friend who's happy to see you, not a drill sergeant tracking your absences.

## What This Means for SpellBound

### Already doing well ✅
- No pressure, no timers, no failure states
- Celebration on completion
- Garden as visual progress (the core differentiator)
- Multiple game types for the same content
- Encouraging messages

### Biggest opportunities 🎯

1. **Adaptive difficulty** (#12 Spaced Repetition) — this is the single highest-impact improvement. Kids who breeze through easy words get harder content. Kids who struggle get more support. The flow channel stays optimal.

2. **Living garden** (#13 Seasonal + creatures) — the garden should feel alive. Seasonal changes, weather, time of day, creatures that appear based on milestones. This is our "variable novelty" engine.

3. **Completion tracking** — show per-list progress: "You've practised 6/8 words this week across 3 different games." Gentle progress, not pressure.

4. **Effortless entry** — the "Today's idea" on the home page could be a one-tap quick-start into a recommended game. "Try Word Scramble with your new science words!"

5. **Character with personality** (#14 Avatars) — a garden companion who reacts to your play. Happy when you visit, shows you new things, occasionally says something surprising.

6. **Challenge mode** (#16) — weekly themed challenges that create gentle structure. "This week's challenge: practise all your spelling words using at least 3 different games." Opt-in, never required.

## The Line We Don't Cross

- ❌ No streaks that punish absence
- ❌ No limited-time events that create FOMO
- ❌ No leaderboards or rankings
- ❌ No scores or percentage-correct displays
- ❌ No notifications or push alerts to the child
- ❌ No "you got X wrong" summaries
- ❌ No randomised loot/rewards
- ❌ No in-app purchases or virtual currency

Everything we build should pass the test: **"Would I be comfortable if my child's teacher saw this?"**
