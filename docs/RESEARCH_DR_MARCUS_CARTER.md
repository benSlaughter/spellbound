# Dr Marcus Carter — Research Notes

Research by Dr Marcus Carter (University of Sydney) and the Sydney Games and Play Lab, relevant to SpellBound's design philosophy.

Carter is Australia's top-ranked Human-Computer Interaction researcher, director of the Sydney Games and Play Lab, and lead investigator on the ARC Future Fellowship project "The Monetisation of Children in the Digital Games Industry." He's also one of the key academic experts in the ABC TV series "Help! My Kid is a Gamer!"

His work sits at the intersection of children, games, money, and harm — exactly the space SpellBound navigates.

---

## Key Research Findings

### 1. Children's Spending in Roblox (2025)

**Study:** "They're Scamming Me: How Children Experience and Conceptualise Harm in Game Monetization"
**Method:** Interviewed 22 children aged 7–14 (and their parents). Gave each child an A$20 debit card to spend however they liked.

**What happened:**
- 12 of 22 children spent their money in Roblox
- 4 bought physical items (bicycle parts, toys, lollies)
- Children value their in-game purchases — they're not mindlessly spending

**But the systems harm them:**
- Children describe monetisation features as **"scams"**, **"cash grabs"**, and **"literally just child gambling"**
- An 11-year-old described navigating nested virtual currencies as **"scary"**
- A 13-year-old, asked how much Robux costs in real money: **"I can't even begin to grasp that"**
- Virtual currencies deliberately obscure real costs — children spend more than they realise
- Random reward mechanics (loot boxes) cause frustration, disappointment, and family conflict
- Parents feel **"overwhelmed and powerless"** to protect their children

**The conclusion is not "ban games."** It's that **monetised random rewards and virtual currencies are not appropriate in children's games.**

---

### 2. Loot Box Bans Aren't Working (2024–2025)

**Study:** "Investigating mobile games' compliance with Australia's 2024 mandatory minimum age classifications scheme"

Australia banned "in-game purchases with an element of chance" for children under 15 in September 2024.

**Carter's findings:**
- Non-compliance is widespread
- Popular Roblox games (Adopt Me!, Blox Fruits, Pet Simulator 99) still sell chance-based items to accounts with ages under 15
- Mobile game age ratings are globally incoherent — one game can have four different age ratings across different app stores
- Self-regulation by platforms and developers has limited effectiveness
- Current legislation is **"not fit for purpose"**

**Why this matters for us:** Even well-intentioned regulation fails when platforms prioritise revenue. The only reliable protection is building it into the design from the start. SpellBound has no monetisation to regulate.

---

### 3. Games Are Crucial for Children (2024)

**Article:** "Online games should not be included in Australia's social media ban — they are crucial for kids' social lives"

When Australia proposed banning under-16s from social media, Carter argued strongly that games should be exempt.

**The evidence:**
- **93% of Australian children aged 5–14** play video games regularly
- **78% of teenagers** say online games help them feel more connected to friends (Pew Research Center)
- **UNICEF review (2024):** video games boost children's wellbeing by making them feel competent, empowered, and socially connected
- **Queensland schools study:** children who learned with Minecraft Education Edition "overwhelmingly identified themselves as better mathematics students"
- Games offer rich spaces for play, identity formation, imagination, and social connection
- For neurodivergent children, game spaces like Autcraft (a Minecraft server) provide crucial safe social environments

**Carter's position:** Games are not the enemy. Predatory monetisation is the enemy. Design matters.

---

### 4. Roblox's Revenue Model (2024)

Some numbers for context:
- Roblox: **380 million monthly active users** worldwide
- **42% of Roblox players are under 13**
- Revenue in 2024: **US$3.6 billion** — mostly from in-game microtransactions
- Australian children aged 4–18 spend an average of **137 minutes per day** on Roblox

This is the scale of the problem. Billions of dollars extracted from children who can't grasp currency conversions and describe the process as "scary."

---

## Carter's Nuanced Position

Carter is emphatically **not anti-games**. His published positions include:

- Games have "demonstrated benefits for children's education, social lives and identity development"
- "Online games should not be included in Australia's social media ban"
- "We do not suggest monetisation features should be barred from children's games" (but they must not harm)
- The way forward is **"safety by design"** — platforms must design with children's safety as a top priority

His framework is simple: **Games good. Exploitation bad. Design for the child, not the shareholder.**

---

## What This Means for SpellBound

Carter's research validates every core design decision:

| Carter's Finding | SpellBound's Response |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| Virtual currencies obscure real value | We have no currency, no economy, no transactions |
| Random rewards harm children | We have no loot boxes, no chance-based mechanics |
| Children value what they earn | Our garden, badges, and achievements are earned through learning |
| FOMO events create anxiety | We have no limited-time events or expiring content |
| Games have genuine educational value | Our entire purpose is learning through play |
| "Safety by design" is the standard | We design for the child's experience, not engagement metrics |

We are what Carter is arguing the industry should be: a children's game designed entirely in children's interests, with zero monetisation, zero manipulation, and genuine educational value.
